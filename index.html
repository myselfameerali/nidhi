Written for

Nidhi

Of Quiet Mornings & Gratitude

Of quiet mornings, genuine feeling, and the gratitude that remains.

A story worth telling

A letter, before everything else

Dear Nidhi,

I have been trying to write this for a long time. Not because I did not know what to say, but because I kept waiting for the right reason to say it. I understand now that there is no right reason. There is only the truth, and the truth is this: you have mattered to me in ways you probably do not know, and I think that truth deserves to exist somewhere outside of me. Permanently. Honestly. Without asking anything of you in return.

This is not a confession. You already know what I felt I told you, and you were kind about it, and I respect you more for that honesty than I can properly say. This is something different. This is the piece that lives after the confession. The quieter, more lasting thing.

What follows is a true story. Not a dramatic one, just an honest account of what it felt like to cross paths with someone whose presence quietly made things better. I have come to believe that one-sided love, held with dignity and eventually released with grace, is still love and still worth recording. Still worth calling by its name.

You are someone whose presence made ordinary mornings feel like they contained something worth waking up for. You are someone whose quiet consistency showed me things about my own character I needed to see. You are someone I am genuinely, completely glad exists in the world not as a possibility, not as a hope, but simply as a person. A friend. Someone I am lucky to know.

This is for you. All of it. With nothing expected in return.

~ Ali

Chapter One

The Geography of a 6 a.m. World

Woh subah jo roshan thi kisi ki wajah se
Na woh jaanti thi, na maine bataya

There is a world that exists before the rest of the world wakes up. It has its own rules, its own quality of light, its own peculiar silence that is not silence at all but the absence of the noise that will come later. The early morning holds something the rest of the day cannot contain a rawness, an honesty, a sense that whatever happens here happens without the performance that daylight demands of everyone.

The gym at that hour is an entirely different place than the gym at noon. At noon it is theater people watching themselves, watching each other, calibrating their visible effort against their imagined audience. But in the first hour of the morning, all of that falls away. What remains is simply a body trying to become a slightly better version of itself. Nothing more is required. Nothing more is offered.

I came to love this hour not because I was made for early rising I was not but because of what I found there. Not what I went looking for. Simply what arrived, in the way that the best things always do: without announcement, without fanfare, without any prior indication that it was going to matter.

She was simply there one morning. And then another. And another. Not arriving dramatically into my awareness, but gradually becoming woven into the familiar texture of those hours the way a melody, heard often enough, stops being background and becomes something you notice yourself listening for.

A Focal Point, Without Knowing

In architecture, a room is designed around a focal point not something that demands the eye, but something that organizes the surrounding space, gives the room its coherence, gives your gaze somewhere honest and natural to rest. The best focal points do not announce themselves. They are simply the truest thing in the room.

She was this, for those mornings. Not loudly. Never loudly. But unmistakably. When she was there, the morning had a center. When she was not, something in the composition of the hour was quietly, indefinably off.

The small specific things stay with me still: the angle of her usual spot in the room. The particular window through which morning light would eventually find her. The unhurried quality of her warm-up as though she had long since made peace with the process and no longer felt any urgency to rush toward results. The way focused effort looked on her face: not strained, not performed, but natural. Like someone doing exactly what they are meant to be doing in exactly the moment they are meant to be doing it.

I am aware these are small things. But I have come to believe that character is assembled entirely from small things and what these assembled, when I attended to them honestly, was a portrait of someone with a genuinely good relationship to her own existence. That is rarer than it sounds. That is worth noticing. That is worth recording.

Chapter Two

The Gradual Noticing

Ehsaas kab aaya, kahaan se aaya
Yeh jaannte tak toh ghar ban chuka tha

I want to be honest about how this began, because honesty is the only thing this whole piece is built from.

There was no single moment of recognition. No morning where I walked in and understood, in some cinematic flash, that something had changed. It was slower than that and, because of its slowness, more certain. A gradual tilting. The kind of thing that, when you look back and try to locate the beginning, finds you unable to because there was no beginning, only a deepening that had already been quietly happening long before you noticed it was.

I noticed her calmness first. Not the calmness of someone who has shut the world out, but the calmness of someone who has made their own interior arrangement with it. She carried herself without effort not indifferent, not performing ease, but genuinely at home inside her own skin. This is uncommon. When you encounter it, it tends to stop you in a way you cannot immediately explain.

I did not plan to feel anything. That is the honest part. It happened in the way things happen when you are not watching for them: quietly, without drama, and therefore without the opportunity to redirect it before it had already found somewhere to settle.

One Side of Something Real

From my side, things became progressively clearer over those weeks and months. I found myself paying more attention, remembering details that would otherwise have passed through unrecorded, looking forward to ordinary moments that held no objective significance to anyone else. I was not doing anything reckless. I was simply becoming more present in those hours and the more present I became, the more clearly I could see something I did not want to see.

I was the only one feeling this.

Not dramatically. Not with any moment of painful revelation. Subtly, gradually, but with increasing clarity in the absence of reciprocal curiosity, in the way things stayed exactly as they were no matter how much time passed. She did not push anything away. She was simply, naturally, entirely herself which meant she was somewhere I was not.

I stayed longer in the hoping than was wise. I want to say that honestly too, because this piece is not interested in making me look more graceful than I was. I held on to something that had no hand extended toward it. I interpreted silence as open space rather than as the answer it actually was. It took longer than it should have to understand that the clearest no is often just the absence of a yes.

The Place Called Maybe

There is a specific kind of hope that lives inside one-sided situations. It is not loud or desperate. It is quiet and very reasonable-sounding. It says: give it time. It says: you might be reading this wrong. It says: not yet. And so you make your home in the maybe. You learn its geography. You furnish it with small moments that could mean something if interpreted generously enough.

I lived there. I am glad I eventually moved out. But I do not regret having lived there, because the feeling that kept me there was real and real feeling, even when it finds no corresponding truth on the other side, is still worth something. Still worth calling by its name.

Chapter Three

The Art of Being Quiet in a Loud World

Shor mein bhi woh khamosh thi
Aur us ki khamoshi sab se gehri awaaz thi

We live in an era that has made loudness a proxy for significance. The most visible person in any room is assumed to be the most important one. We have organized much of our social world around the belief that presence is proportional to projection that the person doing the most to be seen is the one most worth seeing.

She did not seem to know this, or perhaps simply did not care. She moved through those mornings with a quality of being that did not require anyone's acknowledgment to remain whole. She was not performing discipline. She was not performing ease. She was simply there genuinely, completely in the way that very few people manage to be genuinely anywhere.

Quiet, in the sense I mean it, is not the absence of something. It is the presence of a particular kind of sufficiency. The quality of a person who does not need the room to turn toward them in order to feel real. Who can exist fully without the constant external confirmation that most of us have, without realizing it, come to depend on entirely.

Effort Without an Audience

What struck me early before anything else crystallized into feeling was the consistency of her effort in the absence of any reason to perform it. Day after day, the same quality of commitment, with no visible dependency on mood or circumstance or the motivating presence of someone watching. The work was done because the work was worth doing. The rest, for her, seemed beside the point.

Discipline, for most of us, is at least partially social. We work harder when we think someone is watching. We calibrate our visible effort to our imagined audience. To remove that scaffolding entirely and still show up consistently, for your own reasons, because the thing itself matters requires a relationship with your own values that most people are still somewhere in the process of building.

She appeared to have already built it. And watching that, without any ability to tell her what it was doing for me, became its own quiet kind of education.

The Smile That Belonged to the Morning

There is a taxonomy of smiles that no one formally documents but everyone intuitively understands. The smile that is punctuation. The smile that is armor. The smile that is an advertisement for yourself. And then the rare one: the smile that belongs to no particular social function. That arrives not to manage a situation or create an impression but simply because something inside has briefly surfaced into visibility.

Hers was this last kind. When it appeared unhurried, undeployed, arriving when it arrived rather than when it was useful it was not directed at anything specifically. It was the outward trace of some interior condition. A momentary alignment between what was happening inside and what was briefly, honestly visible outside.

On the mornings when I happened to be in the path of it, the air felt different. Not because anything had changed. Because something had been briefly illuminated and then returned, quietly, to its natural state, as if it had never been otherwise. I carry that still. Completely undimmed by everything that came after.

Chapter Four

The Weight of a Nothing

Jo kahaani likhi hi nahi gayi
Use bhool jaana kyun itna mushkil hai

There is a specific kind of exhaustion that comes from mourning something that never had a name.

When a defined relationship ends, the world provides structure for the grief. There are clear before and afters, conversations that mark the transition, ways of accounting for the loss that other people can understand. But when you have to let go of something that existed fully on one side and only as ordinary courtesy on the other there is no structure. Nothing happened, on paper. And yet something did. The gap between the outer nothing and the inner something is, in its own way, a harder thing to carry than a named loss with named weight.

"You don't just move on from a person. You move out of the future you had quietly built for the two of you alone."

That is what I had to release: not just her, but the version of things I had furnished entirely from my own imagination. A quiet future that had never existed anywhere except in the ongoing architecture of my own hope. Letting go of a person is one kind of grief. Letting go of a privately constructed 'maybe' is something different because no one else even knows it existed, and so no one else knows you are giving it up.

It is a quiet eviction. No one sees you leave. But the cold is just as real.

What the Vessel Held

I poured, genuinely and without reservation, into something that was not going to pour back. Not because she was withholding. Not because of timing or circumstance or any of the comfortable explanations that keep the door ajar. Simply because the feeling was not there on her side, and feeling is not something that can be earned or waited into existence.

Understanding this clearly sitting with it honestly rather than around it was one of the more uncomfortable things I have done. It removed every flattering narrative. It was not timing. It was not that she hadn't seen it yet. It was simply a difference. And a difference, I have learned, is not a wrong. It is just a difference.

She did nothing wrong. She was exactly herself consistent, genuine, living her own life rather than managing my feelings about it. That is not a failing. That is just a person being a person, and doing it with integrity.

What I kept, from all of it, is not the disappointment. What I kept is the evidence of my own capacity the proof that I can feel something real, hold it with dignity, carry it without making it someone else's weight. That I can love, quietly and completely, without demanding that the love be returned. These are things worth knowing about yourself. They cost something to learn. I am glad I learned them.

Chapter Five

What Admiration Actually Is

Taarif woh nahi jo munh par ki jaaye
Taarif woh hai jo dil khamoshi mein kare

Admiration is a form of seeing. It is what happens when you look at someone and, in that looking, recognize something genuinely good a quality, a way of being, a kind of wholeness and feel, in the recognition, something that has nothing to do with what you want from them. Admiration is complete in itself, in the act of accurate seeing. It does not require proximity. It does not need reciprocity. It is not organized around the gap between where you are and where you want to be.

What I feel when I think about her now having walked through the full truth of the one-sidedness, having understood clearly what it was and what it was not is admiration. Still. Undimmed. Not despite everything, but in some way because of it. Because now it is no longer entangled with wanting. It has been refined down to its actual substance, which is simply the recognition of something genuinely good.

The Distance Between Wanting and Admiring

Wanting is always in motion. It is oriented toward its object, always conscious of the gap, structured around an absence it is trying to close. There is an ache built into the architecture of wanting the ache of not yet having, of distance not yet closed.

Admiration is different in a fundamental way. In its truest form, admiration is already arrived. It looks at what is and finds in what is something sufficient something that does not need to be anything other than what it already is. It is perhaps the least possessive of all the feelings, which is also why it is one of the most durable. It does not depend on outcome. It does not sour with disappointment. It simply remains, as evidence that something real passed through your life and that you were paying close enough attention to receive it.

Changed by Someone Who Did Not Know

She altered me in ways she will never know about, which is perhaps the most interesting kind of alteration the kind that works precisely because it was not intentional. It happened the way weather happens: not directed at you, but around you, and you discover its effects only later, in who you have quietly become.

More consistent. The example of her showing up, day after day, for her own reasons and no one else's this worked on me slowly, persistently, until it became harder to excuse myself from my own commitments. The example was too close and too real to ignore.

More honest. Having walked through the experience of one-sided feeling sitting with it rather than avoiding it, letting it clarify rather than fester I have grown less tolerant of the comfortable story. I prefer the accurate one now, even when it costs more.

More at peace with things being small. The connection between us was small by every external measure. And yet it was enormously instructive. She taught me that not everything meaningful needs to be large. That the most durable things are often the quietest.

Chapter Six

The Lighthouse That Did Not Know the Ships

Khat likha tha jo bhejna nahi tha
Magar sach tha, is liye likhna zaroori tha

A lighthouse does not know the ships it guides. This is one of the more quietly remarkable facts about them that their work is real and vital and entirely invisible to the one doing it. The light turns, the beam crosses the water, and ships find their bearing in it. The lighthouse, the whole time, simply stands and sends its light and knows nothing of who it is reaching or what it is saving them from.

There are people like this in the world. They do not know they are guiding anything. They do not know that their particular way of being their quiet consistency, their unperformed integrity, their simple honest presence is, for certain people in certain seasons of their lives, a signal in the dark.

She was this for me. Not because I was in crisis, not because my life needed saving, but because there were mornings when the work of becoming who I was trying to become felt harder than others. And on those mornings, the sight of her simply doing her own work quietly, for her own reasons, without audience was not a small thing. It was, in the plainest sense, the thing that kept me moving.

Not because she was performing inspiration. Not because she was trying to be anything to me at all. Precisely because she was doing none of that. She was just living, honestly, with integrity, for herself. And the realness of that the evidence of it, close enough to see clearly was more genuinely motivating than anything that could have been designed to motivate me.

What Consistency Looks Like From the Outside

Consistency is invisible from the inside. From within the practice of showing up every day, it does not feel like discipline or courage or anything worth naming. It just feels like doing what you said you would do. The alarm goes off. You go. This is simply what you do.

But from the outside, consistency is one of the most powerful things a person can embody. It is the visible evidence of an internal commitment holding steady, day after day, regardless of conditions. It is proof that you are, in the most ordinary and reliable sense, the person you intend to be.

Watching her sustain this across weeks and months taught me something I had understood theoretically but had not yet felt in my body: that the accumulation of ordinary days, each one unremarkable, is how anything real is built. That character is not formed in the dramatic moments. It is formed in the Tuesday mornings. In the mornings after hard nights. In the mornings when there is no particular reason to show up except that it is what you do.

She made me better. Entirely without meaning to. That is a generous thing to have done. And it deserves to be said, and meant, and placed somewhere permanent.

Chapter Seven

The Ten-Year Wish

Woh jalti rahi apne liye
Aur mujhe raasta milta raha

Ten years from now, this will still be with me. Not as an unresolved longing, not as something I am still working through, but as a settled and specific part of my own story a chapter I lived fully, understood eventually, and carried forward not as loss but as instruction. As proof of something.

The feeling that lived in those mornings genuine, unhurried, one-sided as it turned out to be was real. I want to say that without apology. It was real. It deserves to be called real, not softened into 'just admiration' or 'just a crush' or any of the diminishing words we use to make one-sided love sound like less than it was. Something happened inside me that was true. The fact that it found no corresponding truth on the other side does not retroactively make it false.

"Ten years from now, if someone asks me to name a time when ordinary mornings felt like something worth waking up for — I will name those."

There is a feeling that does not have a clean name not quite admiration, not quite friendship, something that sits quietly between the two. It is what happens when you find yourself genuinely oriented toward another person's flourishing. When their wellbeing matters to you not because of what it gives you, but simply because it does. When the thought of them, years later, still brings warmth rather than ache.

This is where I am. The warmth is real. The ache is gone. What remains is something that does not require anything from her in order to be complete because it always was complete, held entirely within me. It just took time to understand that completeness does not require reciprocity. That some feelings are whole even when they are held alone.

What Silence Holds

She has taken up a small, permanent residence in my interior life. Not as a fixation that is the wrong word entirely but as a presence. The way a particular place, visited often enough and associated with enough genuine feeling, becomes part of the geography of who you are. You do not think of it constantly. But it is there. It is yours. It is part of what makes the map of your inner life specific rather than anonymous.

She made my silences more particular. More inhabited. She gave them a warmth they did not have before not an ache, but the quiet certainty that something genuinely good happened in this chapter of my life. That I encountered someone whose quality of being reminded me that the world still contains quiet grace, and that quiet grace is worth paying the closest possible attention to.

Chapter Eight

Saadgi The Untranslatable

Das saal baad bhi agar koi pooche
Toh kahoon ga — woh subahein sach mein khoobsurat thin

There is a word in Urdu I cannot translate without losing it. Saadgi. Its nearest English approximations simplicity, plainness do not capture what it actually means. Saadgi is not the absence of complexity. It is the presence of a kind of organic sufficiency the quality of having arrived at your essential form and needing nothing added to it. The beauty of a stone worn smooth by water: all the unnecessary surfaces gone, what remains simply the truest version of itself.

She has saadgi. I mean this not as a compliment in the decorative sense but as a precise description. She does not perform her own existence. She does not ornament or elaborate it for external consumption. She simply is fully, consistently, without the layer of careful self-presentation that most of us have learned, almost without noticing, to keep between ourselves and the world.

In an age that rewards elaborateness more projection, more visibility, more deliberate construction of the image you present saadgi is quietly radical. It is the refusal, conscious or otherwise, to participate in the theater of constant self-performance. It is the confidence to simply exist and let that be enough.

Beauty That Does Not Know Itself

The most genuinely beautiful things tend not to know they are beautiful. This is not a paradox it is a consequence of what makes them beautiful in the first place: the absence of self-consciousness. Beauty that watches itself in a mirror has already introduced a performance into its nature, and performance, however skilled, carries within it a slight hollowness that the honest eye can detect.

Beauty that does not know it is beautiful has a quality of something found rather than made. You encounter it; you do not receive it. It was not waiting for you. It was simply there, being what it is, for reasons that have nothing to do with being seen.

She is this kind. And even now, having walked through everything the hoping, the one-sidedness, the honest reckoning the recognition of this quality in her remains exactly what it always was. Undimmed. Undistorted. Simply true.

This, I think, is the mark of admiration rather than wanting: that it survives disappointment intact. That it does not need the outcome it hoped for in order to remain real. The good thing I saw in her was genuinely good, independent of whether it was ever going to be mine. Seeing it clearly, still, from the other side of all of it that is how I know what I carried was the real thing.

Chapter Nine

What the Mornings Taught Me

Saadgi ko samjhaana mumkin nahi
Bas mehsoos hoti hai — jab ho toh

I want to take honest stock not to count silver linings, but because I genuinely believe that what happens to you is only half the story. The other half is what you do with the understanding that comes after.

Presence Is a Form of Generosity

She was genuinely there in those mornings. Not performing presence, not managing it simply inhabiting it. And this, the bare fact of someone being fully where they are, turns out to be one of the rarer gifts a person can offer a shared space. It raised the quality of those hours for me in ways I did not understand at the time and have only come to understand since. I try now, more than I used to, to actually be where I am. It is harder than it sounds. But I keep trying, because I saw what it looks like when it is done without effort, and I know now that it is worth the work.

A Missing Yes Is the Clearest No

Most of us wait for a definitive moment of rejection a conversation, a clear statement, a door closed with finality before we give ourselves permission to let go. We have decided, collectively, that this is what an ending looks like, and without it we feel we do not have permission to grieve what we are losing.

But the clearest no I have ever received was silence. Not hostile silence just the simple, natural absence of reciprocal curiosity, the effort that never came not because it was withheld but because it simply did not exist. I have learned to read this. To give it the same respect I would give a spoken answer. This is not cynicism. This is just clarity about how feelings actually communicate, which is often not in words at all.

Gratitude Is a Choice, Not a Response

I used to think of gratitude as reactive something that arises in response to receiving what you wanted. These mornings taught me something different. Gratitude can be chosen. It can be the orientation from which you receive whatever is actually in front of you, rather than measuring the distance between what is and what you hoped for.

Every morning I found her there, I could have spent the time aware of everything the situation was not. Instead I chose, increasingly deliberately, to be grateful for what it actually was: a beautiful, unhurried, uncomplicated gift. A few minutes of the world being better than it needed to be. That choice made over and over, until it became something close to a practice has shaped more of how I move through the rest of my life than I initially understood it would.

Chapter Ten

Always

Ustaad woh bhi hote hain
Jinhe khabar nahi ke woh padha rahe hain

Stories end. Feelings do not they change shape, settle into something quieter, find their final form. But they do not end. And what I feel, on this side of everything, is not something I am trying to put down. It is something I have chosen to carry differently.

She is living her life. I am living mine. The geography that once overlapped in those early morning hours has shifted, as all geographies eventually do. A chapter ended not dramatically, not with the kind of finality that marks endings in the stories we tell each other, but in the natural way of things that have found their shape and become what they were always going to be.

What I am carrying forward is not a question. It is an answer. A clear, settled understanding of what those mornings were, what they cost, what they gave, and what I want to do with both the gift and the instruction.

I want to keep caring the way I cared in those mornings quietly, consistently, without making the caring someone else's problem. I want to keep showing up the way she showed up for my own reasons, in the absence of an audience, because the thing itself is worth doing. I want to keep being the kind of person who notices small graces, who recognizes saadgi when it is standing right in front of him, who can hold feeling with open hands rather than a closed fist.

And I want to find, when the time comes, someone who wants to be fully present in my life as much as I want to be in theirs. I know now what that means. I know what its absence feels like which means I know the precise shape of what I am looking for. That is not nothing. That is, in many ways, the most valuable thing those mornings gave me.

"Some people mark your life loudly. Others mark it quietly and you only find the mark years later, in who you became."

She is the second kind. And the mark she left is not a scar. It is the clean evidence of something genuinely good that happened in the ordinary geography of a few unremarkable early mornings, to a person who was paying close enough attention to receive it.

Nidhi if you are reading this, know that this is not a burden. Not a claim. Not a door left open for something I am still hoping for. It is a record of beauty encountered and honestly received. A story of genuine feeling, told with nothing but respect, from someone who is simply glad you exist.

You are someone worth writing ten thousand words about.

You always were.

Aur chhodte waqt bhi shukriya kehta hai
